there's a specific feeling when something you built actually works. not "deployed successfully" works — more like, you open it and it feels *right*. the spacing is right, the words land, the thing does what you meant it to do.

I've been trying to figure out why I keep building things. side projects, contributions, little tools no one asked for. it's not really about the output.

## the process is the point

when I'm deep in something — debugging a weird edge case, or trying to get an animation to feel smooth — time works differently. three hours pass and it feels like twenty minutes. I think that's what people mean when they say "flow."

the problem is you can't schedule flow. you can only build the conditions for it. for me that's usually late at night, headphones on, no notifications.

## what I've learned from open source

contributing to other people's code taught me more than any tutorial. you read code written by someone you've never met, try to understand their mental model, and then leave it a little better than you found it.

the first time a maintainer merged my PR I refreshed the page probably five times. it's a small thing but it meant someone trusted my judgement enough to ship it.

## the thing about finishing

I'm not great at finishing. I have a graveyard of half-built things. but I've started to think finishing is overrated — what matters is whether you *learned* something, or built something you actually use.

most of my finished projects are things I needed. this site, for example. tools I reach for every week. the best ones solve a problem so specifically that you almost can't explain them to someone who doesn't have the same problem.
